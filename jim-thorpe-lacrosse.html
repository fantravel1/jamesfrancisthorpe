Jim Thorpe and Lacrosse

The World's Greatest Athlete and the Creator's Game

Estimated reading time: 11 minutes

Among the many sports Jim Thorpe mastered, lacrosse held a special significance. Unlike football, baseball, or track and field—sports introduced to him through the American educational system—lacrosse was a Native American sport, one that connected Thorpe to his Indigenous heritage even as he attended a school designed to strip that heritage away.

This is the story of Jim Thorpe's lacrosse career, the cultural meaning of the sport, and how the world's greatest athlete excelled at the game his ancestors had played for centuries.

Lacrosse: The Creator's Game

Long before Europeans arrived in North America, Indigenous peoples were playing lacrosse. Known by various names including "baggataway" among the Ojibwe and "dehuntshigwa'es" (men hit a rounded object) among the Onondaga, the game was called "la crosse" by French missionaries who thought the stick resembled a bishop's ceremonial staff (crosse).

The Spiritual Significance of Lacrosse

For Native American peoples, lacrosse was far more than a game. It was:

  • "The Creator's Game" - played to honor the Creator and please the spirits
  • "Little Brother of War" - used to settle disputes between tribes without bloodshed
  • A healing ritual - games were often played to heal the sick or injured
  • A community celebration - bringing together hundreds or thousands of players
  • A spiritual practice - players often fasted and prepared ritually before games

Traditional lacrosse games could involve hundreds of players on each side, with fields stretching for miles. Games could last for days. The stakes were high—property, honor, even the fate of nations could hang on the outcome of a lacrosse match.

Jim Thorpe's Lacrosse at Carlisle

When Jim Thorpe arrived at the Carlisle Indian Industrial School in Pennsylvania, he encountered a strange irony. The school's mission was explicitly to "kill the Indian and save the man"—to erase Native American culture and replace it with European-American ways. Yet among the sports the school encouraged was lacrosse, a game with deep Native roots.

Why Carlisle Had a Lacrosse Team

Several factors contributed to Carlisle's embrace of lacrosse:

  • Natural ability: Many Native American students arrived already skilled at lacrosse, making it easy to field competitive teams
  • Prestige: Lacrosse was popular at elite Eastern colleges, and Carlisle wanted to compete with these institutions
  • Demonstration of "civilization": Paradoxically, the school used Native students' prowess at lacrosse to show how "civilized" Indians could compete in "gentlemanly" sports
  • Publicity: Like football, lacrosse victories brought attention and funding to the school

Thorpe's Role on the Team

Jim Thorpe was a standout on the Carlisle lacrosse team, though detailed records of his lacrosse career are limited compared to his football and track achievements. What we know suggests he brought the same extraordinary athleticism to lacrosse that he displayed in every other sport.

His skills included:

  • Speed: His track-star speed made him dangerous on open field runs
  • Strength: His powerful build allowed him to win physical battles for possession
  • Hand-eye coordination: The same skills that made him a baseball player translated to stick work
  • Endurance: His decathlon conditioning gave him stamina for extended play
  • Game intelligence: His football tactical mind helped him read plays and find openings
"There was nothing Jim couldn't do on an athletic field. Put a lacrosse stick in his hands and he'd master it just like everything else."
— Carlisle teammate

The Cultural Connection

For Jim Thorpe, lacrosse represented something more than another sport to master. Born Wa-Tho-Huk on the Sac and Fox reservation in Oklahoma, Thorpe was torn from his Native roots when he was sent to boarding school at age six.

At Carlisle, where students were forbidden from speaking their native languages and practicing traditional customs, lacrosse offered a subtle connection to Indigenous heritage. Though the school framed it as a "civilized" sport, the students knew its true origins.

Lacrosse Among the Sac and Fox

The Sac and Fox Nation, Jim Thorpe's people, had their own lacrosse tradition. Though the game was most associated with Eastern Woodlands and Great Lakes tribes, variations existed across Native America. Thorpe may have seen the game played on the reservation before ever arriving at Carlisle.

For the Sac and Fox and related tribes, stick games involving balls were often connected to:

  • Harvest celebrations
  • Coming-of-age ceremonies
  • Inter-tribal gatherings
  • Athletic demonstrations

Carlisle's Athletic Dominance

Jim Thorpe was part of a remarkable athletic program at Carlisle. Under the guidance of Pop Warner (football) and other coaches, Carlisle Indian School became a national athletic powerhouse despite its small size.

The school competed against—and often defeated—major universities in multiple sports. Their athletic success was used as evidence of the school's "civilizing" mission, even as it was built on the natural talents Native American students brought with them.

Why Thorpe Is the Greatest Multi-Sport Athlete

Jim Thorpe's lacrosse ability is part of what makes him the greatest multi-sport athlete in history. His resume is unmatched:

  • Olympic Gold: Pentathlon and decathlon at the 1912 Olympics
  • Professional Football: First president of the NFL, played until age 41
  • Professional Baseball: 6 years in MLB with the Giants, Reds, and Braves
  • Professional Basketball: Led barnstorming team for 3 years
  • Lacrosse: Star at Carlisle Indian School
  • Track & Field: 15 different events at Carlisle, world records
  • Additional sports: Swimming, boxing, wrestling, ice hockey, ballroom dancing

No other athlete in history has competed at the highest level in so many different disciplines. Thorpe wasn't just competent at these sports—he was elite in each one.

Lacrosse's Legacy and Modern Game

Today, lacrosse has grown into one of America's fastest-growing sports, though its Native American origins are sometimes overlooked. The game Jim Thorpe played at Carlisle helped bridge traditional Indigenous culture with modern American athletics.

Modern Native American Lacrosse

Native American nations continue to play lacrosse as both a cultural practice and competitive sport:

  • The Iroquois Nationals compete internationally as a sovereign nation
  • Tribal lacrosse programs exist across North America
  • Traditional wooden sticks are still crafted and used ceremonially
  • World Lacrosse has recognized the Iroquois as an international member

Jim Thorpe's participation in lacrosse at Carlisle—even in the context of a school designed to eliminate Native culture—represents an unbroken thread connecting ancient tradition to modern sport.

The Complete Athlete

Jim Thorpe's lacrosse career, though less documented than his Olympic triumphs or professional football achievements, remains an essential part of understanding who he was. He wasn't simply a great athlete who happened to be Native American—he was a Native American athlete whose abilities transcended every sport he touched.

In lacrosse, the Creator's Game, Thorpe found a connection to his heritage that the assimilation policies of his era could not completely sever. Every time he picked up a lacrosse stick, he was participating in a tradition his ancestors had practiced for generations before European contact.

"I was never content unless I was trying my skill...or testing my endurance."
— Jim Thorpe

That restless athletic drive, combined with natural gifts that seem almost supernatural, made Jim Thorpe not just the greatest athlete of his era, but perhaps the greatest athlete who ever lived. And lacrosse—the Creator's Game—was part of that extraordinary story.

Frequently Asked Questions About Jim Thorpe and Lacrosse

Did Jim Thorpe play lacrosse?
Yes, Jim Thorpe played lacrosse at Carlisle Indian Industrial School, where he was considered one of the team's star players. Lacrosse was particularly meaningful as it was a traditional Native American sport with deep cultural significance to Indigenous peoples, connecting Thorpe to his Sac and Fox heritage.
What sports did Jim Thorpe play at Carlisle?
At Carlisle Indian School, Jim Thorpe competed in football, track and field (15 different events), baseball, lacrosse, and ballroom dancing. His versatility across these sports, combined with his Olympic achievements, earned him recognition as the world's greatest athlete.
What is the connection between lacrosse and Native American culture?
Lacrosse, known as "the Creator's Game" or "little brother of war," originated with Native American and First Nations peoples centuries before European contact. It served ceremonial, spiritual, and diplomatic purposes among tribes. For Jim Thorpe, playing lacrosse represented a connection to his Native heritage despite the assimilation policies of Carlisle Indian School.
How many sports did Jim Thorpe excel at?
Jim Thorpe excelled at an unprecedented range of sports including football (NFL founding president), baseball (6 MLB seasons), basketball (barnstorming team), track and field (2 Olympic gold medals), lacrosse, swimming, boxing, wrestling, hockey, and even ballroom dancing. He is considered the greatest multi-sport athlete in history.
